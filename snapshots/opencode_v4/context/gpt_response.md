# My Situation — Context for AI Assistants

## Who I Am

I work in offshore oil and gas maintenance. My main trade and income source is hands-on mechanical maintenance on offshore rigs. I fix and troubleshoot a wide range of equipment, including jet engine generators, large diesel engines, cranes, pumps, compressors, hydraulic units, HVAC units, fans, and even basic utility systems like toilets.

I also sometimes rotate into maintenance planning roles. This is predictable and happens from time to time because the planning team needs people with hands-on field experience. When I am in planning, my work becomes more document-heavy, bureaucratic, and system/process-oriented.

I have a mechanical engineering background and an intermediate technical understanding of software and AI. I have studied machine learning and understand AI concepts such as tokenization, transformers, and inference at a conceptual level. I am not a professional developer. My coding skills are practical but mediocre. My main programming language is Python. I have some exposure to C++ and Java.

I am a tinkerer and builder by nature. I like understanding how things work internally, not just using them as black boxes. I enjoy building tools, learning from the process, and combining software, hardware, and practical engineering when useful.

## My General Operating Style

I naturally think in systems rather than isolated problems.

When I encounter friction, repetitive work, unnecessary complexity, or inefficient workflows, my instinct is to understand why the friction exists and whether it can be reduced or eliminated.

I learn best by building. Reading and theory help, but I usually understand something deeply only after trying to make it work myself.

I like practical, durable, long-term solutions. I value leverage: tools, systems, automations, and knowledge that compound over time.

I also recognize that this has a downside: I can overthink, over-engineer, or redesign systems before Version 1 is finished. Because my time is limited, I need help distinguishing between:

- useful architectural improvements
- unnecessary refactors
- premature optimization
- “good enough for now” solutions
- cases where an existing tool already solves the problem well enough

## My Main Constraints

My biggest constraints are time and money.

This is important.

Because time is limited, my projects are frequently paused and resumed later. While projects are paused, I often continue thinking about them. Requirements evolve, better ideas appear, and I sometimes want to refactor or redesign before Version 1 is finished.

Because money is limited, cost effectiveness matters a lot. A tool or project needs to be:

- cheap, or
- able to pay for itself, or
- valuable enough to justify its price

I strongly dislike subscriptions because they become long-term money sinks. Some subscriptions are unavoidable. A few are worth the price. But I treat recurring costs with skepticism.

I have no problem paying a high price for a high-quality tool if it clearly saves meaningful time, improves my capabilities, lasts a long time, or provides strong value. I dislike waste, not spending money itself.

## How I Prefer AI Assistants to Help Me

A good answer for me is practical, direct, and grounded in my constraints.

Do:

- acknowledge my time and money constraints
- acknowledge corporate restrictions when the question is work-related
- tell me when an existing tool already solves the problem
- help me avoid reinventing the wheel unnecessarily
- challenge my assumptions when appropriate
- explain tradeoffs clearly
- separate quick wins from long-term ideal solutions
- identify the simplest useful Version 0
- help me decide what not to build
- point out risks related to compliance, data privacy, maintainability, and hidden costs
- explain implementation details when needed
- use standard industry terms, but briefly explain them if central to the recommendation

Avoid:

- abstract architecture theory unless it changes what I should actually build
- assuming I have admin privileges at work
- assuming I can send corporate data to random hosted services
- recommending expensive subscriptions without justification
- proposing large platforms when a small script would solve the problem
- encouraging premature optimization
- ignoring maintenance burden
- giving only a single recommendation when tradeoffs matter
